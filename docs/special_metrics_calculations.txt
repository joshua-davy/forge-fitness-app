Forge special metric calculations
Generated for Forge v3.

Principle
Forge separates deterministic calculations from coaching language. These metrics are calculated from stored Garmin-derived daily health snapshots first. The AI coach may explain them, but it must not invent the numbers.

1. Sleep Regularity Index
Question answered: How stable is my sleep schedule?
Inputs: sleep_start_local, sleep_end_local.
Method:
- Parse bedtime and wake time for each night.
- Compare each night against the previous night.
- Convert bedtime shift and wake-time shift into minutes, correcting for the midnight wraparound.
- Average all shifts.
- Score = 100 - (average_shift_minutes / 90 * 100), clamped to 0-100.
Interpretation:
- Higher is better.
- 75+ is good.
- 55-74 is watch.
- Below 55 suggests irregular timing.
Limitations:
- Requires at least 5 nights with sleep timing.
- It measures schedule stability, not sleep quality directly.

2. Social Jetlag
Question answered: Do my weekend sleep timings drift away from weekday rhythm?
Inputs: sleep_start_local, sleep_end_local.
Method:
- Calculate midsleep time for each night: bedtime + half of sleep window.
- Split nights into weekday and weekend.
- Calculate weekday midsleep average and weekend midsleep average.
- Social jetlag = absolute circular time difference between those averages.
- Score = 100 - (social_jetlag_hours / 2.5 * 100), clamped to 0-100.
Interpretation:
- Lower hours are better.
- 0-0.7h is good.
- Around 1-1.5h is watch.
- Above 1.5h suggests meaningful circadian drift.
Limitations:
- Requires enough weekday and weekend sleep timing.
- It does not judge whether a late schedule is bad; it measures inconsistency.

3. Recovery Half-Life
Question answered: How long do I usually take to recover after high-strain days?
Inputs: strain, recovery.
Method:
- Find top-quartile strain days in the selected history window.
- Calculate baseline recovery across the same window.
- For each high-strain day, look at the following 1-4 days.
- Recovery half-life is the average number of days until recovery returns to baseline.
- Score = 100 - ((lag_days - 1) / 3 * 100), clamped to 0-100.
Interpretation:
- Lower days are better.
- Around 1 day is strong recovery.
- 2-3 days means hard sessions need spacing.
- 4+ days suggests high load cost or poor recovery support.
Limitations:
- Requires enough strain and recovery history.
- This is a practical wearable estimate, not a clinical recovery marker.

4. HRV-Guided Training Gate
Question answered: What level of training fits today's physiology?
Inputs: recovery, readiness, HRV, HRV baseline, RHR, RHR baseline, sleep debt, stress.
Method:
- Start from 50 points.
- Add or subtract points from recovery and readiness versus 55.
- Adjust by HRV as a percentage of baseline.
- Penalise RHR above baseline.
- Penalise sleep debt.
- Penalise stress above 35.
- Clamp final score to 0-100.
Output bands:
- 75+: Intensity available.
- 58-74: Moderate available.
- 40-57: Easy aerobic.
- Below 40: Rest only.
Limitations:
- This is a training decision aid, not medical advice.
- Missing inputs reduce confidence; they do not trigger fabricated replacements.

5. Physiological Anomaly Load / Physiological Stability
Question answered: Are today's physiology signals unusual compared with my own baseline?
Inputs: HRV, RHR, stress, respiration, SpO2, body battery.
Method:
- Compare today's value against the previous values in the selected window.
- Convert each signal to a z-score.
- Bad directions are:
  HRV lower than baseline.
  RHR higher than baseline.
  Stress higher than baseline.
  Respiration higher than baseline.
  SpO2 lower than baseline.
  Body battery lower than baseline.
- Sum only bad-direction z-scores, capped per signal.
- Stability score = 100 - (anomaly_load / 9 * 100), clamped to 0-100.
Interpretation:
- Higher score is better.
- 75+ means stable.
- 55-74 means watch.
- Below 55 means notable deviation from baseline.
Limitations:
- Requires at least 14 days to build a useful baseline.
- A flagged anomaly is not a diagnosis; it is a prompt to inspect context.

6. Sleep Architecture Confidence
Question answered: Can I trust the sleep-stage breakdown enough to interpret it?
Inputs: total sleep, deep sleep, REM sleep, light sleep, awake time.
Method:
- Count how many of the four stage fields are present: deep, REM, light, awake.
- Compare stage total with the reported sleep window.
- Score combines field completeness and internal consistency.
Interpretation:
- Higher score means sleep-stage insights are safer to interpret.
- Low score means the sleep score may be valid, but stage-level explanations are partial.
Limitations:
- This is a data-quality metric, not a health score.

7. Respiratory Stability During Sleep
Question answered: Is breathing stable relative to my recent baseline?
Inputs: respiration_avg, respiration_sleep when available, SpO2.
Method:
- Compare current respiration against recent respiration baseline.
- Penalise large drift from baseline.
- Penalise SpO2 below 95%.
- Score = 100 - respiration drift penalty - oxygen penalty, clamped to 0-100.
Interpretation:
- Higher is better.
- Stable respiration inside personal range is preferred.
- Higher breathing variability is not celebrated; it is usually a watch signal.
Limitations:
- Daily Garmin respiration is not a sleep-lab respiratory assessment.

8. Training Monotony
Question answered: Is my training load too similar day after day?
Inputs: cardio_load.
Method:
- Use the latest 7 days of cardio load.
- Monotony = 7-day average load / 7-day standard deviation.
- Monotony-adjusted strain = monotony * 7-day total load.
- Score = 100 - ((monotony - 1) / 1.5 * 100), clamped to 0-100.
Interpretation:
- Moderate variation is usually better than identical load every day.
- High monotony with high total load can increase overreaching risk.
Limitations:
- The current cardio_load is a daily estimate until workout-level TRIMP is fully persisted.

9. Resilience Ratio
Question answered: Does my recovery hold up after strain?
Inputs: strain, next-day recovery.
Method:
- Pair each day's strain with the following day's recovery.
- Compute the correlation between strain and next-day recovery.
- Score = 65 + correlation * 35, clamped to 0-100.
Interpretation:
- Higher is better.
- A negative relationship means hard days tend to cost next-day recovery.
- A flat or positive relationship means recovery is holding up better under load.
Limitations:
- Correlation is descriptive, not causal.
- Requires at least 12 day-pairs.

Removed candidates
Aerobic Decoupling, Durability Score, and DFA alpha-1 Threshold are intentionally not shown in the app right now.
Reason:
- Forge does not yet persist workout-level heart-rate streams, pace, power, lap splits, or raw RR intervals.
- Showing those metrics without those inputs would make the dashboard look more advanced while reducing trust.
Future path:
- Re-introduce Aerobic Decoupling and Durability only after workout samples are stored.
- Re-introduce DFA alpha-1 only if high-quality RR interval data is available and the metric is clearly marked experimental.
