Forge dashboard review against professional health apps
Date: 2026-05-21

Reference apps reviewed
- WHOOP: recovery, strain, stress, sleep need, sleep schedule, behaviour guidance, personal baselines.
- Oura: readiness, sleep, resilience, daytime stress, medium-term recovery/stress balance.
- Garmin: training readiness, HRV status, sleep score, acute load, stress history, Body Battery.
- Bevel: recovery, sleep, strain, stress, cardio load, cardio focus, HR recovery, biological age, AI coaching, correlations, share/export surfaces.

What Forge does well now
1. Clear section architecture
Forge now separates Home, Fitness, Biology, Sleep, Focus, and Profile. This is stronger than a single metric wall and closer to how premium apps split "today", "training", "body", and "sleep".

2. Direction-aware metrics
Forge now understands that higher is not always better. RHR, stress, sleep debt, biological age, respiration drift, and social jetlag are treated differently from HRV, SpO2, recovery, and VO2 max.

3. Good one-glance visual language
The dark mobile-first dashboard, age hero cards, rings, vertical range markers, and colour semantics are visually coherent. The app now feels closer to a premium consumer health product than a developer dashboard.

4. Useful Focus concept
The new Focus section is important. Professional coaching apps do not simply show data; they triage what matters. Forge now starts to rank poor markers, small improvements, and strong points.

5. Honest data-limited behaviour
Removing aerobic decoupling, durability, and DFA alpha-1 from the UI was the right call until workout streams are persisted. This protects trust.

6. Profile/export direction
The Profile section creates a base layer for future accounts, sharing, identity, and metric normalisation. This aligns with premium products that make health snapshots portable and explainable.

What Forge should improve next
1. Persist workout-level activity streams
This is the biggest gap versus Garmin, WHOOP, Bevel, and serious coaches. Forge needs per-workout HR, pace, power, cadence, duration, sport, lap/split data, and ideally RR intervals. Without this, cardio load, sport freshness, HR recovery, decoupling, discipline half-life, and durability are only approximate.

2. Add true historical focus tracking
The Focus section currently infers priorities from current metric states and trends. The next step is a focus_history table: metric, issue, start date, action plan, weekly check-ins, resolved date, and improvement evidence.

3. Add explainability drawers
Every score should have "How this is calculated" inside the UI, not only in docs. Users need to see inputs, weights, missing data, confidence, and why the metric changed.

4. Add behaviour correlation controls
Bevel-style insight systems expose correlations but remind users that correlation is not causation. Forge should show r-values with a plain-English explanation, sample size, confidence, and "what to test next".

5. Improve coach memory
Coach output is now useful, but it should remember goals, past focus areas, completed actions, and user preferences. This should be deterministic state first, AI wording second.

6. Add medium-term resilience view
Oura's Resilience is explicitly medium-term. Forge's Physiological Stability is daily against baseline. Add a separate 14-30 day resilience trend that balances stress load, restorative time proxies, sleep quality, HRV, RHR, and recovery.

7. Better readiness colour bands
Garmin's Training Readiness bands make 1-24 red/poor, 25-49 orange/low, 50-74 green/moderate, 75-94 blue/high, 95-100 purple/prime. Forge should keep its own style but use similarly explicit bands for Recovery, Readiness, and Training Gate.

8. Export and sharing polish
The new Profile image export is a start. Add share cards for Recovery/Sleep/Strain/Fitness Age and user-configurable privacy toggles.

9. Calibration states
Professional apps tell users when a metric is calibrating or data-limited. Forge should show "calibrating", "partial data", and "requires workout samples" as first-class states.

10. Multi-user architecture
The product eventually needs users, encrypted Garmin sessions, profile tables, per-user metrics, and login sessions. The current local setup is fine for a personal cockpit but not a product userbase.

Current product judgement
Forge is strongest as a decision cockpit: the visual direction, section split, focus system, and direction-aware scoring are moving in the right direction. The remaining gap is less about visual polish and more about data depth, longitudinal coaching memory, and explicit explainability.

Source notes
- WHOOP publicly frames its product around sleep, strain, stress, recovery, baselines, sleep need, and guidance that translates data into recommendations.
- Oura Resilience is a medium-term balance of physiological stress and recovery over recent days rather than a single-day score.
- Garmin Training Readiness combines sleep score, recovery time, HRV status, acute load, sleep history, and stress history with explicit score bands.
- Bevel's public help describes behaviour insights as statistical correlations and includes newer app areas such as Biological Age, Health Records, Fitness charts, and AI coaching.
